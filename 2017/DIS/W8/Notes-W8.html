Distributed Intelligent Systems

Multi-Level Modeling Methodology

Modeling Choices

  • Gray-box approach: to easily incorporate a priori information (e.g., # of agents, technological and environmental features)
  • Probabilistic: to capture noisy interactions, noisy robotic components, stochastic control policies, and enable aggregation schemes towards abstraction
  • Multi-level: to represent explicitly different design choices, trade off computational speed and faithfulness to reality, bridge mathematically tractable models and reality in an incremental way
  • Bottom-up: start from the physical reality and increase the abstraction level until the highest abstraction level

Modeling scales

  • Submicroscopic: Webots
  • Microscopic: non spatial, state = behaviour, exact model in terms of quantities
  • Macroscopic: non spatial, mean field approach, ODEs, approximations, …

Experimental Invariant Features and Modeling Assumptions

  • Short-range (typically 1 robot diameter), crude (noisy, a few discrimination levels) proximity sensing
  • Full mobility but limited navigation (no planning, no absolute localization)
  • Limited use of long-range communication channels available on the platforms (only as a teammate sensor)
  • Reactive, behavior-based control, with a few internal states
  • No overcrowded arenas
  • Multiple runs (typically 5+) for the same experimental parameters; randomized robot poses at the beginning

Semi-Markovian assumption : The system future state is a function of the current state.

Spatiality assumption: use non-spatial for collective performance.

Theoretical Background

Microscopic Level

Let \(p(n,t)\) = the probability of an agent to be in state \(n\) at time \(t\).

If Markov properties are fulfilled:

\[\Delta p(n,t) = p(n,t+\Delta t)-p(n,t)\]

\[= \underset{n'}{\sum} p(n,t+\Delta t | n', t) p(n', t) - \underset{n'}{\sum} p(n', t+\Delta t | n, t) p(n,t) \]

\[=\text{inflow} - \text{outflow}\]

where the inflow is the sum of possible states \(n'\) the agent can be in. Under Markov assumption this is the probability of a transition from \(n \rightarrow n'\) \(\times\) the probability the agent was in a given state \(n'\).

Macroscopic level

Average over the total number of agents and bring \(\Delta t \rightarrow 0\) to get ODE.

Time continuous

\[\frac{d N_n (t)}{dt} = \underset{n'}{\sum} W(n|n',t)N_{n'}(t) - \underset{n'}{\sum}W(n'|n,t) N_n(t)\] (rate equation)

where \(N_n\) is the mean number of agents in state \(n\) at time \(t\), and \(W\) is the transition rate:

\[W(n|n',t) = \underset{\Delta t \rightarrow 0}{lim} \frac{p(n,t+\Delta t | n', t)}{\Delta t}\]

Time discrete

\[N_n((k+1)T) = N_n (kT) + \underset{n'}{\sum} TW(n|n', kT) N_{n'}(kT) - \underset{n'}{\sum} TW(n'|n, kT) N_{n}(kT)\]

where \(k\) is the index, \(T\) is the sampling interval/time step, \(TW\) is the transition probability per stime step.

Discrete vs Continuous:

  • Assess what’s the time resolution needed for your system performance metrics (if time step chosen appropriately small, no impact on prediction accuracy in the type of experiments presented)
  • Choose whenever possible the most computationally efficient model: time-discrete less computationally expensive than emulation of continuity (e.g., Runge-Kutta, etc.)
  • Advantage of time-discrete models: a single common sampling rate can be defined among different modeling levels

Choice of timestep: Measure the interactions (delay states, transition times, …). Consider average values. Choose the Greatest Common Factor as time step (e.g. \(3\) s obstacle avoidance, \(4\) s object manipulation, \(T = 1\) s)

Methodology

  1. Target system + tasks (define the environment, the tasks)
  2. Metrics and State Space (performance metrics, state space)
  3. Submicroscopic level (faithful implementation of the design choices in e.g. Webots)
  4. Microscopic Level (aggregate local interactions and reduce intra-robot details)
  5. Macroscopic Level (aggregate nodes into one or multiple representations e.g. castes at a collective level)
  6. Parameter calibration (decreases with abstraction level, calibrate one level on the underlying one. Use ad-hoc, system ID or statistical methods)
